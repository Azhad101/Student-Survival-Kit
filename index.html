3.1  What is Federalism?

Federalism is a system of government in which power is divided between a central authority and various constituent units of the country. Usually, a federation has two levels of government, though it is not uncommon to have more than two levels.

One is the government for the entire country, usually responsible for a few subjects of common national interest, such as defence, currency, and foreign affairs. The others are governments at the level of provinces or states, which look after much of the day-to-day administration of their region.

3.2  What Makes India a Federal Country?

The Constitution of India originally provided for a two-tier system of government: the Union Government, representing the country as a whole, and the State Governments. Later, a third tier was added in the form of Panchayats and Municipalities. Federalism in India has thus evolved into a genuinely three-tier structure, as it exists today in countries like Brazil and Belgium.

Under Federalism, no single level of government can override the powers of another simply by passing an ordinary law. Any change to this basic division requires the consent of both the Union and a specified proportion of State Legislatures — a safeguard written into the Constitution to prevent the misuse of power by any one tier.

3.3  How is Federalism Practised?

The mere existence of two or more levels of government in a country does not necessarily make it federal in spirit — this has to be examined case by case. True Federalism has been strengthened in India through linguistic reorganisation of states, the growing role of coalition politics, and greater recognition of demands for autonomy.

Students should note that decentralisation, or the transfer of power from central and state governments to local governments, is regarded by many scholars as a natural extension of the federal principle rather than a separate idea altogether.
